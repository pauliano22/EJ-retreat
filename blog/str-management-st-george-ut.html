St. George's Short-Term Rental Market: A Southwest Hotspot

St. George sits at the gateway to some of Utah's most stunning scenery, making it a magnet for outdoor adventurers, snowbirds escaping harsh winters, and families on road trips. This diverse visitor base creates demand that stretches well beyond a single season, giving STR investors a meaningful edge over markets tied to just one travel window.

Popular neighborhoods and zones for short-term rentals in the area include the Snow Canyon corridor, the Entrada and Sunbrook communities near the golf courses, and properties within easy distance of downtown St. George and its growing restaurant and arts scene. Homes with private pools, hot tubs, or sweeping red-rock views typically command premium nightly rates.

  • Proximity to Zion National Park drives heavy spring and fall demand
  • Winter snowbird traffic sustains occupancy through the cooler months
  • Sand Hollow Reservoir and Quail Creek attract warm-weather water sports visitors
  • St. George's PGA-level golf courses draw destination golfers year-round
  • Sports tournaments and events at Dixie Convention Center create recurring booking spikes

Revenue Potential for Short-Term Rentals in St. George, Utah

St. George is widely regarded as one of the stronger STR revenue markets in the Mountain West. Many operators report average daily rates that climb significantly during peak seasons — particularly spring wildflower season near Zion, fall foliage months, and holiday weekends — with well-appointed homes sometimes achieving ADRs well above regional averages for comparable long-term rentals.

Seasonal demand in St. George is more balanced than many resort markets. While spring and fall typically represent peak booking windows, the mild winters attract snowbirds and the summer months draw water sports enthusiasts to Sand Hollow, keeping occupancy from falling off a cliff in the off-season. Properties with standout amenities like private pools, outdoor kitchens, or game rooms typically outperform comparable listings throughout the year.

Professional management can meaningfully close the gap between what a self-managed property earns and its true revenue ceiling. Dynamic pricing alone — something most self-managers handle manually or skip entirely — can lift revenue by capturing surge demand during events and high-traffic weekends that casual owners routinely under-price.
