The Big Bear Lake Short-Term Rental Market: What Investors Need to Know

Nestled at 6,752 feet in the San Bernardino Mountains, Big Bear Lake attracts a uniquely diverse guest base — weekend skiers from the Los Angeles basin in winter, summer boaters and hikers, and fall leaf-peepers in between. That multi-season demand is a rare advantage that many STR markets simply cannot offer, and it translates into occupancy that many operators report staying well above the national average for mountain destinations.

Popular neighborhoods and pockets include the Village area for walkability, the North Shore for quieter lakefront access, and the Moonridge corridor close to Bear Mountain and Snow Summit ski resorts. Each micro-location carries different nightly rate potential and guest profiles.

  • Strong demand from the 20-million-person Los Angeles–San Diego drive market
  • Twin ski resorts anchor a reliable November–March peak season
  • Summer lake recreation extends high-demand weekends well into September
  • Short-term rental regulations in Big Bear Lake are established, giving investors relative regulatory clarity compared with many California cities

Revenue Potential for Short-Term Rentals in Big Bear Lake

Well-managed short-term rentals in Big Bear Lake can be among the more lucrative mountain assets in California. Typical cabins and chalets in desirable locations often see average daily rates ranging from roughly $250 to $500 or more on peak winter and summer weekends, with annual gross revenue that many operators in comparable mountain markets report landing between $50,000 and $120,000 depending on size, amenities, and location.

Seasonal patterns matter enormously. Winter ski season and major holiday weekends — Thanksgiving, Christmas, New Year's, Presidents' Day — typically command the highest ADR. Summer lake weekends and Fourth of July are close behind, while shoulder months like May and October still generate meaningful occupancy from hikers, couples, and remote workers seeking mountain air.

Self-managing owners in Big Bear Lake frequently leave revenue on the table through static pricing, slow guest responses, and inconsistent cleaning quality that damages review scores. Do I need a permit or license to operate a short-term rental in Big Bear Lake?

Yes — the City of Big Bear Lake and San Bernardino County both require short-term rental permits and collection of Transient Occupancy Tax (TOT) from guests. Requirements include a valid STR license, TOT registration, and compliance with occupancy and noise rules.
